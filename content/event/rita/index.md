summary: Scientific management of free-roaming dogs for eliminating human rabies deaths.
abstract: "To achieve the global goal of "Zero by 2030" by eliminating human rabies deaths requires
an equitable strategy that focuses on regions where canine rabies is endemic and where
free-roaming dog populations are high. Communities in these regions often face
challenges related to healthcare access and rabies prevention efforts, making it essential
to address these barriers for effective and sustainable outcomes. While mass dog
vaccination and ensuring access to life-saving rabies biologics for dog bite victims are
essential strategies for reducing human rabies deaths, these alone are not enough to
achieve the "zero by 2030" goal. To be truly effective, the approach must be rational,
sustainable, and, most importantly, equitable. Human rabies deaths disproportionately
affect those in economically disadvantaged communities, who often bear the brunt of
living with large free-roaming dog populations but remain voiceless in the fight against
the disease. Therefore, any strategy aimed at eliminating human rabies deaths must
prioritize these vulnerable populations to ensure fairness and long-term success.
Effective control measures must focus on mass dog vaccination of owned dogs, and
sustainable, locally relevant solutions for managing free-roaming dog populations. This
includes community education, specifically focusing on responsible pet ownership and
curbing unscientific practices like feeding of unowned dogs in public spaces. Another
critical issue that needs attention is the problem of parachute science and activism,
where external groups often promote unscientific practices, such as Animal Birth
Control for free-roaming unowned dogs. These interventions are frequently
implemented without understanding the local context or considering evidence-based
approaches, leading to ineffective or even counterproductive outcomes. A one-size-fitsall
approach is unlikely to work; strategies need to be adapted to local socio-cultural
contexts to ensure long-term success and fairness in rabies control."
